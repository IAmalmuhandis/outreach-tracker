Documents & materials to carryMust-haves
  • One-page YARE brief — what it is, the problem it solves, pricing tiers or "get in touch" CTA
  • A printed or ready-to-show portfolio of sample translations (English → Hausa, with context)

Research to do before each meetingPer-org prep
  • Look up the organisation's most recent project or campaign in Nigeria (mention it early)
  • Find the name of the specific person you're meeting — confirm spelling and title
  • Know one concrete pain point they've had with translation or localisation in the past
  • Check their vendor procurement process — are they open to pilot projects or do they need formal registration?
  • Look up if they've posted any translator job ads recently (Malaria Consortium have — use it)

How to present yourselfIdentity & presence
  • You are a founder, not a salesperson. Lead with mission — "we're building language infrastructure for Africa" — not features.
  • Dress in smart Nigerian attire (traditional or fitted African wear) where culturally appropriate. At INGOs like GIZ or USAID, business casual or a clean traditional ensemble both work. At tech hubs, smart casual is fine. At government offices, err conservative.
  • Introduce yourself fully: "I'm [name], founder of YARE — a platform connecting African language translators and voice artists with organisations that need them." Always say your full name and your city of origin (Kano) when relevant — it builds trust with northern-facing organisations.
  • Don't open with "I have a startup." Open with the problem: "I noticed your organisation regularly commissions Hausa translations — I wanted to share what we've built to make that easier."
  • Speak with quiet confidence. You know the problem better than anyone in that room. You've lived it.
  • Be specific and brief. Have your one-liner ready: "YARE is a marketplace where organisations post translation and voice-over jobs, and verified native-speaker professionals deliver them — faster and cheaper than traditional agencies."
The approach phasesStep by step
1
Book the meeting (don't walk in cold to senior people)
Email + LinkedIn request + WhatsApp where available. Reference something specific: "I saw your recent Hausa campaign for [project] and wanted to discuss how YARE can support future work." For reception-level staff at INGOs, walking in with a brief is acceptable — ask for the Communications or Procurement officer.
2
Open by naming their world
In the first 60 seconds: reference something real about their work. "Your polio campaign in Sokoto last year — how did you handle the Hausa translation?" Show you've done your homework. People immediately relax when they realise you understand their context.
3
Listen more than you talk
Ask: "What's your current process for Hausa translation?" Then be quiet. Their answer tells you exactly how to pitch YARE. Don't interrupt. Write down what they say.
4
Pitch to the gap they just revealed
After they've described their problem, say: "That's exactly the gap YARE closes." Then explain how, specifically. You've now made them feel heard AND solved their problem.
5
Ask for a trial, not a contract
"Can we handle your next Hausa translation piece — no cost, no commitment — so you can see the quality firsthand?" Low-stakes yes. High-value proof. Trial projects almost always convert.
6
Leave with WhatsApp
Before you leave: "What's the best way to follow up with you — WhatsApp or email?" Get their WhatsApp number if they offer. In Abuja, this is where relationships actually move.

Likely objections & how to handle themBe ready
"We already have translators we work with."
Good — YARE doesn't replace your translators, it organises them. If they're already on our platform, you manage them there. If not, we can onboard them. Either way, your procurement process gets simpler and the quality layer is built in.
"How do we know the quality is good?"
That's the right question. Every translator on YARE goes through a native-speaker vetting process, and every translation has a human QA review. We also offer a free trial project — let us handle one piece and judge the quality yourself before any commitment.
"We use Google Translate / DeepL."
Generic tools perform poorly on Hausa and almost not at all on Fulfulde. They don't understand dialect, cultural reference, or register. For campaign communication where trust matters — health, civic messaging — a machine error in Hausa can actively undermine your programme. YARE uses AI to accelerate, but every output is reviewed by a human native speaker.
"We don't have budget right now."
Understood — we're not here for a contract today. We're here to start a relationship. When your next campaign cycle opens and you need Hausa content, YARE should be the first name you think of. Can I leave you our brief and follow up in a few weeks?
"We need to go through our procurement process."
Absolutely — that's exactly why we're here now, before you need it urgently. What does your procurement process look like? We can start registration now so that when the need arises, we're already approved and ready to go.
"How do we know YARE will still be around?"
Fair concern. We're Abuja and Kano-based, actively building, and we've designed YARE to serve Nigerian institutions specifically — not as a side project. Here's our founder's contact directly. You're not dealing with an agency; you're dealing with the person who built this.
"Can you do [language X] too?"
Our current depth is in Hausa and Fulfulde, with Yoruba and Igbo in development. If you have an immediate need in another language, tell us — we may already have translators in that language in our network, or we can onboard them for your project.
Within 24 hours of every meetingFollow-through
  • Send a WhatsApp (preferred) or email thanking them by name and referencing one specific thing they said
  • Attach the YARE one-pager if you haven't already shared it
  • Post a brief LinkedIn/X reflection: "Had a fascinating conversation today about [topic] — here's what I learned." Tag the organisation if appropriate. This builds public credibility while the meeting is fresh.
  • Log the meeting in a simple tracking sheet: person, org, what they said, next step, follow-up date
  • If they asked for something (more info, a sample translation, a deck) — deliver it within 48 hours, not a week
How to keep relationships warmLong game
  • Follow up every 3–4 weeks if no active conversation — share a relevant article, a YARE milestone, or a question: "Thinking of you — are you planning any Hausa campaigns this quarter?"
  • Send something of value before you ask for something. A relevant news piece, a translation tip, an introduction to someone useful in your network.
  • Track their campaign cycles. INGOs budget annually. Government agencies have procurement windows. Know when they're planning and show up before their need is urgent.
  • When you do a successful trial job, ask for a one-line testimonial in writing. These become social proof for every subsequent pitch.
  • Build a WhatsApp broadcast list of all warm contacts. Monthly update: a YARE milestone, a case study, a piece of content — keep YARE top of mind without pestering individuals.
